Science Communication Survey Research Guide

This guide describes the methodology for creating a survey that links popular science video content to peer-reviewed academic literature.

Video Selection Methodology

Select videos from a science education channel sorted by total view count to identify the most impactful and audience-resonant topics. Aim for diversity across scientific disciplines: at least one video from physics, one from mathematics, one from biology or evolution, one from cognitive science or psychology, and one from engineering or applied science. Avoid selecting multiple videos on the same narrow topic.

Paper Matching Methodology

For each selected video, identify the core scientific concept from the video title and description. Use that concept as a search query against the academic paper database. Search strategies that work well include using technical terms such as "quantum decoherence" rather than "quantum physics" and "evolutionary game theory" rather than "evolution". Select papers published within three years of the video publication for maximum relevance. Prefer papers with clear abstracts that explain the connection to the video topic.

Document Structure

The survey document should follow this structure. Begin with a title heading and an Executive Summary of 200 words explaining the purpose, methodology, and key findings. Follow with five topic sections, each with a level 1 heading naming the scientific domain. Each topic section should contain a paragraph describing the video with its title and view count, and a second paragraph explaining the related academic paper with the lead author name and paper title. End with a Conclusion section of 150 to 200 words and a References section listing all papers in the format: Author Last Name, First Initial. (Year). Paper Title. arXiv:ID.

Writing Style

Write in a style that is accessible to an educated general audience while maintaining academic rigor. Avoid jargon without explanation. When citing view counts, round to the nearest hundred thousand. When referencing paper abstracts, paraphrase rather than quote directly.
